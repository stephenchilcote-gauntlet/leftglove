The Planes

Five layers of any system

  • 0 Reality — the world
  • 1 Observation — what we know
  • 2 Orientation — what we care about
  • 3 Design — how we'll fix it
  • 4 Artifact — the thing itself

Plane 0

Reality is indirect

You cannot directly mutate Plane 0.

You pick up a stone, learn to throw it, aim, chuck it, and see what happens.

All your interactions with the world are experiments.

It's a credit to our built models that we so easily forget that.

Plane 1

The plane of infinite facts

Inexhaustible — literally infinite. No point in even trying to catalogue it all.
Provenance — every fact has a source, a scope, and a duration.
Superseded — facts expire. Later observations replace earlier ones.
Incomplete — anything you know is represented here, and it's always partial.

The Core

Users. Misfit. Systems.

Users — something that has goals. People, organizations, other systems. We call them users when they're at the boundary of a system.
Misfit — the gap between what a user wants and what the world provides. The world pushes back.
System — a set of forms and processes that bridge that misfit. That is their entire purpose.
Stakeholders — not everyone is a user. Stakeholders care about the system's outcomes but don't interact at the boundary. Regulators, executives, the dev team itself. A system that serves stakeholders over users is a perverted system.
Composes — a microservice is a system. A Kubernetes cluster is a system. Netflix is a system. The boundary moves; the definition holds.

Plane 2 — Orientation

The only thing you care about

Which misfits, for which users, will we solve?
We might be wrong on any of these. But this is our working hypothesis.

This is it. Everything else — every metric, every process, every tool — is derivative of this.

If you can't at least budge this, your system is a ghost town. No one cares.

Planes 3 & 4

Design → Artifact

Plane 3 — Design — the blueprint. Architecture, data model, user flows on a whiteboard. An experimental design. Not the experiment itself — you're deciding what to build, and in what shape.
Plane 4 — Artifact — the built thing. Code on a server, an app in someone's hand. Where most people think the work happens — but it's the narrowest kind. The kind an LLM does more of every week. The interesting decisions were made above.
Not always top-down. You often start at Plane 4 and backfill upward — looking for gaps, contradictions, redundancies. This isn't just a build sequence. It's a diagnostic tool.

Bugs Have Planes Too

Where does the bug actually live?

Plane 4 — It doesn't work
Good idea. I want to use it. But I physically can't. The thing is broken.
Plane 3 — It's stupid
It works. But it makes you jump through hoops. The workflow is dumb. People make desire paths through your software because the paved ones don't go where they need.
Plane 2 — Nobody cares
It works fine. Nobody wants it. Big feature, big fanfare, no adoption.
Plane 1 — Wrong from the start
Shows up as a 2, 3, or 4 bug. The observations were wrong. Everything downstream inherited it.

The Loop, Not the V

A greenhouse, not a factory

A factory ships and moves on.

A greenhouse watches real users do the thing — sees what changed in the world since the last deployment — and iterates.

You're not just verifying the old build. You're feeding the next one.

In Practice

Most orgs mash the planes together

Even the best teams squish Planes 1, 2, and 3 into a single PRD.

Some bigger orgs can verify Plane 2 and 3 assumptions. But it's rarely regular or properly segregated.
